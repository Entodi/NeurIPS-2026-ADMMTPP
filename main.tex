\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Alzheimer's disease and related dementias (ADRD) develop over many years, and the most informative measurements change with disease stage~\citep{self2023emerging}.
Molecular and fluid biomarkers tend to be most informative in early stages, while structural imaging, cognitive testing, and functional assessments become more informative later on.
ADRD also differs widely across patients, with subtypes that vary in clinical presentation, progression pattern, and rate of decline~\citep{young2018uncovering,fereshtehnejad2015new}.
Predictive models therefore have to handle both shifts over time in which modalities matter most and large variability between patients.

Much of the machine-learning work in this area still uses a cross-sectional setup, in which separate models are trained for separate outcomes from a fixed historical window, for example diagnosis at 24 months or ADAS-Cog at 36 months~\citep{marinescu2019tadpole}.
This setup does not make full use of the relationships between observations collected over time, and it does not naturally adapt to a patient's disease stage.
Two recent lines of work offer partial answers.
Marked temporal point processes (MTPPs), including neural and LLM-coupled variants, give generative models for irregularly timed event data~\citep{kong2025language,liu2025tpp,li2026longrange,berghaus2026incontext}.
Separately, autoregressive transformers trained on tokenized electronic health records support arbitrary-horizon forecasting from a shared sequence backbone~\citep{renc2024zero,renc2025foundation}.
To our knowledge, these two lines have not been joined into a single generative framework for multimodal ADRD trajectories.

We present an autoregressive generative framework that combines a pretrained language-model backbone, point-process structure for irregular timing, and multimodal ADRD event representations under a single token-prediction objective.
Each patient history is serialized as a modality-tagged, delimited token sequence, and a pretrained autoregressive backbone is adapted to it with parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
In this setup, the conditional density of the next event is captured implicitly through the next-token distribution over that event's serialized tokens, and we show that next-token cross-entropy on the resulting serialization corresponds to a discretized MTPP negative log-likelihood up to tokenizer resolution.
A single model, trained with continued pretraining followed by supervised fine-tuning, handles diagnosis classification, biomarker regression, inter-visit time prediction, and multi-step trajectory generation through the same next-token decoding step, without task-specific backbones.
Because predictions are conditioned on the full serialized history, the model can adjust the effective weighting of modalities to each patient's current stage.
Within this framework we compare three tokenization granularities, visit-level, modality-level, and atomic-event, to map the trade-off between context length and per-event supervision density at an otherwise identical backbone and training objective, and we evaluate a set of optional auxiliary heads (Hawkes intensity, Cox partial hazard, ordinal diagnosis) as different ways of shaping the learned representation; none of the auxiliary heads is used for generation.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=2pt]
    \item A generative representation learning framework for longitudinal clinical records in which a pretrained LLM backbone parameterizes an MTPP through autoregressive token prediction, with a formal link from token-level cross-entropy to the MTPP log-likelihood (§\ref{sec:problem}).
    \item Three tokenization variants, visit, modality, and atomic-event, that trade context length against supervision density at otherwise identical backbone, adaptation, and training objective (§\ref{sec:tokenization}).
    \item An empirical study on two ADRD cohorts from different sources, the TADPOLE subset of ADNI~\citep{adni,marinescu2019tadpole} and the tertiary-care EHR dataset EDUCATE~\citep{arnrich2024medical}, jointly ablating tokenization granularity and a suite of auxiliary-head objectives (Hawkes NLL, Cox partial likelihood, ordinal regression) on the backbone's hidden states (§\ref{sec:regularization}) at fixed backbone, adaptation, and training recipe.
    The present paper reports diagnosis prediction on TADPOLE; the remaining TADPOLE tasks and the EDUCATE cohort are laid out to set the scope of the formulation and are left for future work (§\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Three lines of prior work frame our approach: neural temporal point processes coupled to language models, LLM fine-tuning on natural-language ``pseudo-notes'' rendered from structured EHR, and non-language EHR tokenizers that train sequence models from scratch on custom event vocabularies.
We treat them in turn and state where our variants sit relative to each.

\subsection{Neural and LLM-coupled TPPs}

Marked temporal point processes have long been the standard tool for irregular event streams, with neural parameterizations of the conditional intensity developed over the last decade.
More recently, several works have tied TPPs to language-model backbones.
LanguageTPP~\cite{kong2025language} and TPP-LLM~\cite{liu2025tpp} cast next-event prediction as next-token prediction over a serialized event stream, using an LLM either to emit event distributions directly or to feed an intensity head on top of its hidden states.
MM-TPP~\cite{li2026longrange} extends this line to multi-modal marks, and OpenFIM~\cite{berghaus2026incontext} proposes a foundation intensity model trained on a broad mixture of heterogeneous event streams.
Our work sits in the LLM-coupled family but is distinguished by (i) a controlled ablation over \emph{tokenization granularity} at fixed backbone, adaptation, and training objective, and (ii) treatment of auxiliary heads (intensity, hazard, ordinal) as optional representation regularizers (§\ref{sec:regularization}) rather than primary outputs, so that the same model answers both discrete and time-valued tasks by next-token prediction.

\subsection{LLM Fine-Tuning on EHR Pseudo-Notes}

A parallel line of work renders tabular EHR records into natural-language-like sequences (``pseudo-notes'') and fine-tunes a general-purpose LLM on the resulting text.
MEME~\cite{lee2025clinical} is representative: patient events are templated into prose and the LLM is trained to predict downstream clinical outcomes.
Pseudo-note approaches inherit the linguistic priors of the backbone but pay a cost in numeric fidelity (scalar biomarkers become loosely formatted text) and in point-process structure (event boundaries and inter-event times are diluted into prose).
We keep events as explicitly delimited, modality-typed token strings with dedicated time tokens, which (a) preserves a clean event grid for the MTPP log-likelihood surrogate of Section~\ref{sec:problem} and (b) exposes per-event supervision to the loss.

\subsection{Non-Language EHR Tokenizers}

Independent of LLMs, several works tokenize clinical events into a purpose-built vocabulary and train a transformer from scratch on the resulting stream.
ETHOS~\cite{renc2024zero} encodes patient timelines into a discrete ``health language'' and trains a generative transformer for outcome prediction at arbitrary horizons; ARES~\cite{renc2025foundation} extends this generative-EHR line and is evaluated on similar prediction tasks.
These models share our autoregressive view of a patient record but forgo the linguistic priors of a pretrained LLM and re-learn tokenization, model, and task heads together.
Our variants retain the pretrained LLM via 4-bit quantization and LoRA adapters, so the only trainable structure specific to EHR is the vocabulary extension and the (optional) auxiliary heads.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

We treat each patient's longitudinal record as a realization of a generative, marked temporal point process (MTPP) and learn it by autoregressive token prediction with a language-model backbone.
Section~\ref{sec:problem} develops the general formulation and prediction tasks.
Section~\ref{sec:tokenization} describes three concrete ways to implement this formulation as token sequences, and Sections~\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:training} specify the shared backbone and two-stage training recipe used for all three.
Section~\ref{sec:regularization} introduces the optional auxiliary heads (Hawkes, Cox, ordinal) attached to the backbone's representation.
Datasets, feature schema, and experimental protocol are described in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

Let $\mathcal{S} = \{(t_k, \mathbf{x}_k)\}_{k=1}^{K}$ denote a patient record with $K$ observations, where $t_k \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is the time since baseline (months) and $\mathbf{x}_k$ is a heterogeneous, possibly partially observed mark comprising diagnostic labels, cognitive scores, neuroimaging summaries, and structured codes.
Time-invariant covariates (sex, years of education, APOE-$\varepsilon$4 allele count) are collected in a context vector $\mathbf{c}$; age is treated as a per-visit feature.
We write $\Hcal_k = \{(\mathbf{c}, (t_1, \mathbf{x}_1), \ldots, (t_k, \mathbf{x}_k))\}$ for the history up to and including the $k$-th observation and $\tau_k = t_k - t_{k-1}$ for the inter-observation interval.

A marked temporal point process is characterized by the conditional density of the next event given the history~\citep{daley2003introduction,shchur2021neural},
\begin{equation}
    p^*(t_{k+1}, \mathbf{x}_{k+1} \mid \Hcal_k)
    \;=\; \lambda^*(t_{k+1}, \mathbf{x}_{k+1}) \,
          \exp\!\Bigl(-\!\!\int_{t_k}^{t_{k+1}}\!\!\int \lambda^*(s, \mathbf{x})\,d\mathbf{x}\,ds\Bigr),
    \label{eq:mtpp}
\end{equation}
with conditional intensity $\lambda^*(\cdot \mid \Hcal_k)$.
Iterating the chain rule, the joint density of a full record factorizes into per-event conditionals,
\begin{equation}
    p^*\!\bigl(\{(\tau_k, \mathbf{x}_k)\}_{k=1}^{K} \,\big|\, \mathbf{c}\bigr)
    \;=\; \prod_{k=1}^{K} p^*(\tau_k, \mathbf{x}_k \mid \Hcal_{k-1}),
    \label{eq:mtpp_joint}
\end{equation}
so the MTPP negative log-likelihood of a record is the sum of the per-event log-densities of Eq.~\eqref{eq:mtpp}.

Rather than parameterize $\lambda^*$ directly, we serialize the record into a token sequence via a deterministic, delimited map
\[
    \phi \colon \bigl(\mathbf{c},\, \{(\tau_k, \mathbf{x}_k)\}_{k=1}^K\bigr)
    \;\longmapsto\;
    s_{1:L} \;=\; \bigl(s^{(c)},\, s^{(1)},\, \ldots,\, s^{(K)}\bigr),
\]
where $s^{(c)}$ encodes the static context and each event block $s^{(k)}$ encodes the inter-event time $\tau_k$ and mark $\mathbf{x}_k$ of visit $k$ (Section~\ref{sec:tokenization}).
An autoregressive language model factorizes $s_{1:L}$ by the chain rule, which we regroup by event blocks:
\begin{equation}
    p_\theta(s_{1:L})
    \;=\; \prod_{\ell=1}^{L} p_\theta(s_\ell \mid s_{<\ell})
    \;=\; p_\theta\!\bigl(s^{(c)}\bigr) \prod_{k=1}^{K} p_\theta\!\bigl(s^{(k)} \,\big|\, s^{(c)}, s^{(<k)}\bigr).
    \label{eq:ar}
\end{equation}
Because $\phi$ is injective on its image, each event-block factor $p_\theta\!\bigl(s^{(k)} \mid s^{(c)}, s^{(<k)}\bigr)$ is a parametric surrogate for the MTPP conditional $p^*(\tau_k, \mathbf{x}_k \mid \Hcal_{k-1})$, with continuous marks resolved to the tokenizer's numeric precision.
Token-level cross-entropy on $s_{1:L}$ therefore minimizes a discretized negative log-likelihood of Eq.~\eqref{eq:mtpp_joint}~\citep{liu2025tpp,kong2025language}.
This reformulation (i)~reuses a pretrained language-model backbone in place of a bespoke intensity network, (ii)~handles heterogeneous marks (categorical, ordinal, and continuous) under a single objective, and (iii)~unifies classification, regression, and forecasting as conditional next-token prediction.

\subsection{Three Granularity Variants}
\label{sec:tokenization}

All variants share a structural template for each patient:
\begin{equation}
    \bos \;\; \mathbf{c} \;\;
    \underbrace{\soe \; T(\tau_k) \; E(\mathbf{x}_k) \; \eoe}_{\text{event } k} \;\;
    \ldots \;\;
    \eos,
    \label{eq:template}
\end{equation}
where \soe/\eoe\ are abstract event delimiters, not learned vocabulary items: in implementation they are realized as newline boundaries plus the encoding-specific event prefix that opens each $E(\mathbf{x}_k)$ block (an event index $e_k\mid$ for (V), a modality tag $\langle g\rangle\mid$ for (M), a feature tag $\langle\text{feat}\rangle\mid$ for (A)).
The tokenizer already treats the newline as a separator, so no custom delimiter tokens are added to the vocabulary; the only added items are the modality/feature tags and the task prompts ($\ttoken{TASK\_*}$, $\ttoken{h}$, etc.).
The three variants differ in how the event payload $E(\mathbf{x}_k)$ is decomposed and, as a secondary consequence, in which continuous covariates can cleanly enter an event (the modality-level and atomic-event encodings restrict events to measurements with a natural modality/feature tag, so quantities such as age and raw intracranial volume are dropped in those two variants).
The three variants trade off context length against the density of supervision (fraction of tokens under a cross-entropy loss whose prefix varies across visits).
Let $F$ denote the number of observed feature dimensions at visit $k$ and $G$ the number of modality groups: for TADPOLE, $G$ covers imaging, cognitive, and diagnosis; for EDUCATE, $G$ covers diagnosis, lab, medication, and procedure.
Figure~\ref{fig:granularity} shows the same two-visit history under each variant.

\paragraph{(V) Visit-level encoding.}
One event per visit.
$E(\mathbf{x}_k)$ is a single row containing all observed feature--value pairs for that visit.
This is the most compact representation: a 10-visit TADPOLE patient uses roughly 10 rows, fits comfortably in a 4{,}096-token window, and preserves all same-visit correlations at the cost of coarse per-event supervision.

\paragraph{(M) Modality-level encoding.}
One event per modality group observed at the visit.
$E(\mathbf{x}_k)$ is split into $G$ sub-rows sharing a timestamp, each carrying only the features of its group (e.g., an imaging sub-event with MRI summaries, a cognitive sub-event with MMSE/CDRSB/ADAS).
Missing modalities are omitted rather than flagged, yielding a native handling of block-structured missingness.

\paragraph{(A) Atomic-event encoding.}
One event per individual measurement.
$E(\mathbf{x}_k)$ is decomposed into $F$ single-feature events of the form \ttoken{feat}$\;|\;t\;|\;v$.
This gives the finest per-token supervision (one loss boundary per measurement) and the cleanest treatment of feature-wise missingness, at the cost of $F{-}1$ extra timestamp repetitions per visit.

Numerical values are emitted as text at the backbone tokenizer's native resolution; categorical labels (diagnosis, ICD/SNOMED codes) use a small set of added vocabulary items that we insert into the tokenizer and initialize from the mean of existing embeddings.
Task prompts are appended after the history as $\ttoken{TASK\_*} \, \ttoken{horizon}$ so a single model answers all five tasks and multiple horizons from a shared backbone.

\subsection{Model and Adaptation}
\label{sec:model}

We use a causal language model $f_\theta$ as the backbone.
Unless stated otherwise, $f_\theta$ is Qwen3.5-0.8B, loaded in 4-bit NF4 quantization; scale-up to Qwen3.5-2B is reserved for follow-up and reported only when stated.
For variants that introduce new tokens (category labels, modality tags, horizon bins), we extend the tokenizer and resize the embedding and output-projection matrices; new rows are initialized to the mean of the pretrained rows.

Trainable parameters are restricted to (i)~Low-Rank Adaptation \cite{hu2022lora} inserted in the attention and MLP projections ($\texttt{q,k,v,o\_proj}$, $\texttt{gate,up,down\_proj}$) as $W = W_0 + (\alpha/r)\, B A$ with $r=16$, $\alpha=64$, dropout $0.1$, and (ii)~the new token rows and the language-model head.
This reaches roughly $0.5\%$ of the backbone's parameters and fits on a single A100 80\,GB.

\subsection{Two-Stage Training}
\label{sec:training}

All three variants follow the same two-stage recipe, differing only in the token stream produced by their $E(\cdot)$.

\textbf{Stage~1 (continued pretraining).}
For each patient with $K$ observations we emit every prefix of length $2,\ldots,K$ as an independent sequence (sub-sequence augmentation), which expands $\sim$1{,}700 TADPOLE patients into $\sim$10{,}000 sequences.
The objective is next-token cross-entropy over the full sequence:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{S1}}(\theta)
    \;=\; -\frac{1}{L} \sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \log p_\theta(s_\ell \mid s_{<\ell}).
    \label{eq:loss_s1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Stage~2 (task supervised fine-tuning).}
For each valid split point we construct prompt--response pairs $(P, R)$ where $P$ is the tokenized history followed by $\ttoken{TASK\_*}$ and a horizon token, and $R$ is the target.
Examples across the five tasks are mixed.
The loss is computed only on the response tokens:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{S2}}(\theta)
    \;=\; -\frac{1}{|R|} \sum_{\ell=1}^{|R|} \log p_\theta(r_\ell \mid P, r_{<\ell}).
    \label{eq:loss_s2}
\end{equation}
We study two regimes: \emph{next-visit} ($R$ targets only the immediate next observation) and \emph{horizon-conditioned} ($R$ targets any future observation whose offset matches the horizon token in $P$, so one model answers arbitrary horizons).
Both stages use learning rate $10^{-4}$ and per-device batch size $4$ on a single A100 80\,GB; Stage~1 runs for 7 (no augmentation) or 9 (augmentation) epochs, Stage~2 for 4 or 5 epochs respectively.
Augmentation, when enabled, applies 10\% modality dropout, 2\% feature-value noise, and 15\% history dropout to the training sequences.
Remaining hyperparameters are in Appendix.

\subsection{Auxiliary-Head Objectives}
\label{sec:regularization}

The token-level objective of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:loss_s1}--\eqref{eq:loss_s2} treats inter-event time only through its surface form: the tokens of $T(\tau_k)$ appear in the loss exactly as any other symbol, and nothing in the objective forces the backbone's hidden states to encode time continuously or to respect the ordinal structure of diagnosis.
To probe which, if any, of these inductive biases help, we attach one \emph{optional} auxiliary head on the backbone representation during Stage~2.
All heads share the combined objective
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} \;=\; \mathcal{L}_{\text{S2}} + \gamma\, \mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}},
    \label{eq:combined}
\end{equation}
with $\gamma$ linearly warmed from $0$ to $0.1$ over one epoch so that the text objective is established before the auxiliary head contributes gradients.
Auxiliary heads are trained jointly with the text head but are \emph{not} used for generation; they shape the hidden states only through back-propagation.
Ablations compare each tokenization variant across these heads, including a pure-text baseline ($\gamma=0$), at otherwise matched configurations.

\paragraph{Hawkes intensity head.}
Attached on top of a tokenizer variant that additionally prepends summary temporal features (inter-event statistics) to the prompt.
Small per-mark projections $(\mu_m, \alpha_m, \beta_m) = \mathrm{softplus}\bigl(W_{\{\mu,\alpha,\beta\},m}\, h_k\bigr)$ from the backbone's last hidden state $h_k$ at event positions parameterize a piecewise Hawkes intensity in the classical exponential-kernel form~\citep{hawkes1971spectra,shchur2021neural},
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_m(t \mid \Hcal_k)
    \;=\; \mu_m + \!\!\sum_{j:\, t_j < t,\; m_j = m}\!\! (\alpha_m - \mu_m)\, e^{-\beta_m (t - t_j)},
    \label{eq:hawkes}
\end{equation}
and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}}$ is the MTPP negative log-likelihood
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}}
    \;=\; \int_0^{T} \sum_m \lambda_m(s)\, ds \;-\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log \lambda_{m_k}(t_k),
    \label{eq:nll}
\end{equation}
computed in closed form: the compensator decomposes into a baseline term $T\sum_m \mu_m$ and a sum of per-event excitation integrals $\frac{\alpha_m - \mu_m}{\beta_m}\bigl(1 - e^{-\beta_m (T - t_j)}\bigr)$~\citep{ogata1981lewis}.
The implementation follows the \texttt{PiecewiseHawkesIntensity} interface of OpenFIM~\citep{berghaus2026incontext} but does not share code with it.

\paragraph{Cox proportional-hazards head.}
A log-hazard scalar $f(h_k)$ read from the last hidden state at each event position is trained under the Cox partial log-likelihood~\citep{cox1972regression},
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{Cox}}
    \;=\; -\!\!\sum_{i:\, \delta_i=1}\!
          \Bigl[\, f(h_{k_i}) \;-\; \log \!\!\!\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_i)}\!\!\! \exp\!\bigl(f(h_{k_j})\bigr) \,\Bigr],
    \label{eq:coxph}
\end{equation}
where $\delta_i=1$ marks an observed class transition and $\mathcal{R}(t_i)$ is the risk set at time $t_i$.
Within-batch ties are handled by the Breslow approximation~\citep{breslow1972contribution}.
Two sub-variants are considered: an accumulated-risk-set form that buffers negatives across mini-batches~\citep{katzman2018deepsurv,kvamme2019time}, and an attention-pooled form in which $f$ reads an attention-weighted average over the history's hidden states rather than the last one, using the self-attentive pooling of~\citet{lin2017structured}.

\paragraph{Ordinal head.}
Diagnosis labels admit a natural order CN\,$<$\,MCI\,$<$\,AD, so we additionally consider a two-logit ordinal head that minimizes cumulative binary cross-entropy on the thresholded predictions $P(d > \text{CN} \mid h_k)$ and $P(d > \text{MCI} \mid h_k)$, following the cumulative-link ordinal formulation of~\citet{frank2001simple} and its deep-network version~\citep{niu2016ordinal}.
This head carries no timing information; it tests whether an order-aware classification signal, independent of point-process structure, suffices to shape the hidden states.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Both datasets follow the same abstract schema $(t_k, \mathbf{x}_k)$ with $\mathbf{x}_k$ structured as modality-typed sub-vectors, so the three model variants of Section~\ref{sec:tokenization} apply uniformly; dataset-specific feature lists are deferred to Appendix.
Prediction tasks are defined per dataset, as the two sources differ in the targets available.

\paragraph{TADPOLE (ADNI).}
The TADPOLE challenge subset of ADNI \cite{adni,marinescu2019tadpole} provides $\sim$1{,}700 patients in the training split (D1), each with at least two visits, and a held-out rollover split (D2).
Three static covariates $\mathbf{c}$ (sex, years of education, APOE-$\varepsilon$4 allele count) are emitted once per patient.
Nine observation fields are available per visit: current age, diagnosis $d \in \{\text{CN}, \text{MCI}, \text{AD}\}$, three cognitive scores (MMSE, CDRSB, ADAS-13), three MRI volumes normalized as ratios to intracranial volume (ventricles, hippocampus, whole-brain), and ICV itself in milliliters.
The (V) encoding emits all nine; the (M) and (A) encodings drop age and ICV-in-mL (they have no clean modality/feature tag) and emit the remaining seven grouped by modality or atomized by feature.
Time since baseline $t$ (months) and inter-visit interval $\Delta t$ are attached as tokens at each event position.
Preprocessing is light: raw MRI volumes are read from the ADNI FreeSurfer release, the three normalized volumes are computed as ratios to ICV, ICV is converted from mm$^3$ to mL, ADNI transition labels (e.g., \texttt{NL to MCI}, \texttt{MCI to Dementia}) are collapsed to the 3-way CN/MCI/AD taxonomy, and patients with fewer than two visits are dropped.
Given a history $\Hcal_k$ and horizon $h>0$, we define five prediction tasks on TADPOLE: diagnosis at $h$ (3-way CN/MCI/AD), ADAS-Cog at $h$, ventricle-to-ICV ratio at $h$, inter-visit interval $\tau_{k+1}$, and multi-step trajectory generation by ancestral sampling.
Experiments in this paper report diagnosis; the remaining four are reserved for future work.
We also consider a \emph{DX-hidden} ablation in which the diagnosis field is dropped from each visit event at training and inference, to measure how much of the TADPOLE signal passes through the cognitive and MRI fields alone.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
TADPOLE diagnosis is evaluated with 5-fold stratified group $k$-fold cross-validation on D1 (groups = patient IDs, seed~42), so that no patient appears in both train and validation.
Each fold runs the full two-stage recipe (§\ref{sec:training}) and scores the held-out patients by balanced class accuracy (BCA) over the three classes \{CN, MCI, AD\}; BCA is used as the primary metric because ADNI is strongly class-imbalanced and random-chance accuracy would otherwise dominate a plain accuracy score.
The D2 rollover split is held out as a secondary, temporally shifted test set.
Across tokenization, prompt style, DX-hidden, auxiliary head, next-visit vs.\ horizon regime, and augmentation on/off, the Stage-2 sweep reaches 192 training runs per fold (48 mode-loss pairs $\times$ 2 augmentation settings $\times$ 2 regimes).

\paragraph{EDUCATE.}
EDUCATE is a tertiary-care electronic health record dataset covering dementia and related disorders, harmonized to OMOP concepts and MEDS events \cite{arnrich2024medical}.
Each patient has an irregular stream of diagnosis, laboratory, medication, and procedure events with absolute timestamps.
Four prediction tasks are defined on EDUCATE: binary healthy/non-healthy classification on the full history, five-way class (AD, MCI, FTD, DLB, other) restricted to non-healthy patients, inter-visit interval $\tau_{k+1}$, and multi-step trajectory generation.
Because diagnosis codes directly encode the class labels, the two classification tasks are additionally evaluated with a \emph{leak-safe} preprocess that removes all dementia/ADRD ICD-10 prefixes (G30, G31, F00--F03, $\ldots$) and SNOMED/text mentions at load time; gaps between the two preprocess variants quantify code-level leakage.

\paragraph{Decoding for diagnosis.}
For the diagnosis task reported here, we decode greedily at temperature $T_{\text{gen}}=0.05$ with constrained generation over $\{\text{CN},\text{MCI},\text{AD}\}$, conditioned on the tokenized history followed by the task prompt $\ttoken{TASK\_DX}\,\ttoken{h}$.
The same prompt template is reused across horizons so that a single trained model handles all horizon queries.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We study how the tokenization granularity of §\ref{sec:tokenization} and the auxiliary-head choices of §\ref{sec:regularization} affect TADPOLE diagnosis prediction.
All three encodings (V, M, A) share the same backbone, adapter, and two-stage training recipe, so any gap in BCA traces to how the event payload is encoded.
We then run each encoding under each of the auxiliary heads (pure text, Hawkes NLL, Cox partial likelihood with accumulated-risk-set and attention-pooled sub-variants, and ordinal cumulative BCE), at otherwise matched settings, to test which inductive bias on the hidden states transfers to next-token diagnosis accuracy.

\section{Auxiliary Heads}
\label{app:aux_heads}

This appendix gives the formal definition of each auxiliary head of §\ref{sec:regularization}, matched to the \texttt{brainx} implementation.
Notation: $h_k \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the backbone's last hidden state at event position $k$; $t_k$ and $m_k$ are the event time and mark; $d_k \in \{0,1,2\}$ encodes diagnosis (CN, MCI, AD); $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the logistic function; $\mathcal{B}$ is a mini-batch of valid event positions.
The pure-text baseline sets $\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = 0$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:combined}, so Stage~2 optimizes only the response-token cross-entropy $\mathcal{L}_{\text{S2}}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss_s2}; the subsections below cover the five heads that specify a non-trivial $\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}$.

\subsection{Hawkes NLL (closed form)}
Small per-mark projections produce $(\mu_m^{(k)}, \alpha_m^{(k)}, \beta_m^{(k)}) = \mathrm{softplus}\bigl(W_{\{\mu,\alpha,\beta\},m}\, h_k\bigr)$ at every event position $k$.
With the piecewise exponential intensity of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hawkes}, the compensator admits the closed form
\begin{equation}
    \int_0^T \lambda_m(s)\, ds
    \;=\; \mu_m^{(K)}\, T
    \;+\; \!\!\sum_{j:\, m_j = m}\!\!
          \frac{\alpha_m^{(j)} - \mu_m^{(j)}}{\beta_m^{(j)}}
          \Bigl(1 - e^{-\beta_m^{(j)}(T - t_j)}\Bigr),
    \label{eq:hawkes_compensator}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_m^{(K)}$ is the baseline read off the last event.
Summing over marks and subtracting the log-intensity at events yields $\mathcal{L}_{\text{NLL}}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:nll}~\citep{hawkes1971spectra,ogata1981lewis}.

\subsection{Cox partial log-likelihood}
A scalar log-hazard $f(h_k) = w^\top h_k + b$ is read off the last hidden state at each event position, with $w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}$.
Events marking an observed class transition are labeled $\delta_k = 1$, otherwise $\delta_k = 0$.
With times sorted descending and within-batch ties resolved by the Breslow approximation~\citep{breslow1972contribution,cox1972regression},
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{Cox}}
    \;=\; -\frac{1}{N_\delta}\!\! \sum_{k:\, \delta_k = 1}\!\!
    \Bigl[\, f(h_k)
    \;-\; \log \!\!\!\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_k)}\!\!\! \exp\!\bigl(f(h_j)\bigr) \,\Bigr],
    \qquad N_\delta = |\{k: \delta_k = 1\}|,
    \label{eq:cox_app}
\end{equation}
where the risk set $\mathcal{R}(t_k) = \{j : t_j \geq t_k\}$ is evaluated via a descending-time cumulative log-sum-exp.

\subsection{Cox with accumulated risk set}
At small per-batch sizes the ranking signal in Eq.~\eqref{eq:cox_app} weakens.
Following~\citep{katzman2018deepsurv,kvamme2019time}, we buffer $\{f(h_k), t_k, \delta_k\}$ across $N_{\text{acc}} = 4$ consecutive micro-batches and compute Eq.~\eqref{eq:cox_app} on the combined buffer, yielding an effective risk set of $|\mathcal{B}| \cdot N_{\text{acc}}$.
Gradients flow only through the most recent micro-batch; earlier entries are detached and provide ranking context.
On non-flush steps, only the text loss is applied.

\subsection{Cox with attention-pooled hidden state}
A learned query $q \in \mathbb{R}^d$ attends over the full history $\{h_1, \ldots, h_K\}$ via scaled dot-product attention,
\begin{equation}
    \alpha_k
    \;=\; \frac{\exp\bigl(\langle q, h_k \rangle / \sqrt{d}\bigr)}
               {\sum_j \exp\bigl(\langle q, h_j \rangle / \sqrt{d}\bigr)},
    \qquad
    \bar h
    \;=\; \sum_k \alpha_k\, h_k,
    \label{eq:attn_pool}
\end{equation}
after which the log-hazard is $f(\bar h) = w^\top \bar h + b$, following the self-attentive pooling of~\citet{lin2017structured}.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cox_app} is then applied on the attention-pooled log-hazards.

\subsection{Ordinal cumulative BCE}
A two-logit head $g: h_k \mapsto (z_{k,1}, z_{k,2}) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ parameterizes cumulative probabilities
$P(d_k \geq 1 \mid h_k) = \sigma(z_{k,1})$ and $P(d_k \geq 2 \mid h_k) = \sigma(z_{k,2})$
under the cumulative-link ordinal formulation~\citep{frank2001simple,niu2016ordinal}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{ord}}
    \;=\; \frac{1}{2|\mathcal{B}|} \sum_{k \in \mathcal{B}}\!
    \Bigl[\,
      \mathrm{BCE}\!\bigl(z_{k,1},\, \mathds{1}[d_k \geq 1]\bigr)
    + \mathrm{BCE}\!\bigl(z_{k,2},\, \mathds{1}[d_k \geq 2]\bigr)
    \,\Bigr].
    \label{eq:ord}
\end{equation}
Posterior class probabilities used at decoding time are recovered as $P(\text{CN}) = 1 - \sigma(z_1)$, $P(\text{MCI}) = \sigma(z_1) - \sigma(z_2)$, $P(\text{AD}) = \sigma(z_2)$.
